How to Choose the Right Scale for an Architectural Model

Scale is one of the first — and most consequential — decisions in any model project. The right choice keeps the model legible, practical to transport, and powerful in the room.

Choosing the right scale for an architectural model depends on the model's purpose, the level of detail required, and the actual dimensions of the real object. The guidelines below are useful rules of thumb, but every project deserves its own evaluation.

Start by calculating the model's size

Before selecting a scale, work out how large the finished model will physically be. A model that becomes unusually tall, sprawling, or tiny is a clear sign the scale needs adjusting. The goal is a piece that's comfortable to view at arm's length and easy to move and display.

Common scales — and what each is for

These ranges cover the majority of architectural work. Actual dimensions always override these guidelines.

Interiors, units, hotel rooms1:50
Building architecture1:100
Mixed-use blocks, TODs1:200
Masterplans, large sites1:500
City-scale overview1:1000

How we recommend a scale

When we review a project, scale selection is guided by a few practical factors:

  • Purpose — what decision the model needs to support, from concept massing to a sales-gallery centerpiece.
  • Level of detail — finer façade, glazing, and landscaping detail reads better at a larger scale.
  • Viewing distance — a model seen across a gallery has different needs than one studied around a table.
  • Real dimensions — the size of the building or site ultimately sets the practical bounds.
The right scale ensures clarity, practicality, and visual impact. If you're unsure, send us your dimensions and intended use — we'll recommend a scale during review.
